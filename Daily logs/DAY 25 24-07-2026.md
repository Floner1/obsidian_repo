Introduction, final

The problem with real estate data in Ho Chi Minh City isn't that it doesn't exist. It's that nobody's put it in one place. There's no MLS-equivalent, no central registry you can check to see what actually sold for what. So firms fall back on relationships built up over years, and asking prices that can sit far apart from what a deal actually closes at.

Part of why nobody's automated this yet: most of the raw data lives in Vietnamese, and the analytics tools built for this region either skip it or scrape a thin slice of it through English aggregators. The listings exist. They're just written in a language most tooling can't read.

This research looks specifically at the residential side, apartments, houses, land, and villas, not the commercial and office space Avison Young's research team spends most of its time on. It pairs a scraper built for Vietnamese-language residential listings with the judgment of people who work the broader market every day at Avison Young, to see where the two run into the same problems and where they don't.

Methodology, final

I built a scraper using requests and BeautifulSoup that pulls listings directly from Vietnamese-language sources. alonhadat.com.vn is the primary source and live. homedy.com is the planned secondary source and isn't built yet. Each listing gets deduplicated on an exact match of its source site and source ID, no fuzzy matching across sources, so the same unit reposted under a new ID shows up as a separate row. That's a deliberate limitation, not an oversight.

batdongsan.com.vn was tested first and dropped. Plain requests hit a 403 Cloudflare challenge immediately, a headless browser never cleared it, and even a full browser session got through the first page and then got blocked on every navigation after that, tested with gaps as long as 20 seconds between requests. Clearing that challenge would mean making automated traffic pass as human, and that's off the table on principle, not just difficulty. batdongsan stays in the repo only as a manual fallback, saved HTML files fed in by hand, never scheduled and never counted on for volume.

Requests to the live sources are rate limited with a randomized delay of one to three seconds, randomized specifically so the traffic doesn't fall into an even, machine-like rhythm a site could fingerprint. A failed request gets three retries at two, four, and eight seconds before it's logged and skipped, and a 429 response gets read for its Retry-After header instead of a guessed wait time.

Everything lands in Postgres, gets served through a Django REST API, and shows up on a paginated, filterable dashboard built with Django templates and Tailwind. Two of three anomaly rules run today, both simple threshold checks against a raw field, not machine learning: a listing gets flagged if it's been active more than 90 days, or if it has fewer than three photos. The third rule, price_gap, would compare a listing's price against a scikit-learn model's prediction, but that model isn't built yet, so the rule doesn't run on anything real for now.

One thing worth saying plainly instead of burying it: an English-speaking competitor can't replicate this data access. Most of the actual listing volume in this market runs through Vietnamese-language sources, and most firms doing analytics here either can't read them or never bothered building a scraper for them. Being able to read and write Vietnamese isn't incidental to this project. It's what makes the full data pool reachable in the first place.

Findings, final

SaiPrice tracks residential listings only, apartments, houses, land, and villas, scraped from alonhadat.com.vn. The database holds 844 active listings right now, up from 93 when the anomaly rules were first checked against live data back in mid July. The pipeline is moving fast.

Only 3 of those 844 listings are flagged anomalous. stale_listing sits at zero, and that's a real number, not a bug. Scraping only started around July 10, so almost nothing has had time to sit on the market past the 90 day threshold yet. A near zero stale count says something about the pipeline's age, not about the market itself. price_gap still isn't live, it needs the ML model that hasn't been built. That leaves low_photos as the only rule that could explain the 3 flagged listings, though the current scored and flagged breakdown against all 844 hasn't been pulled yet, so that attribution is a likely explanation, not a confirmed one.

The category split is the real headline here. 842 of 844 active listings are apartments. Land has one listing, house has one, villa has zero. On listing intent, 843 are for sale, and only 1 is for rent. Right now this isn't a residential market dataset in the way it sounds, it's an apartment for sale dataset with a handful of stragglers everywhere else. Any claim about "the residential market" in this report needs that attached, or it's overstating what these 844 rows actually cover.
